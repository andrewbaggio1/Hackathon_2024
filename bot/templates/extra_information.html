Efficient Market Hypothesis

The Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH) suggests that asset prices fully reflect all available information. This means that it is impossible to "beat the market" consistently on a risk-adjusted basis since stock prices already incorporate and reflect all relevant information. EMH is categorized into three forms:

  • Weak Form: Prices reflect all historical information.
  • Semi-Strong Form: Prices reflect all publicly available information.
  • Strong Form: Prices reflect all information, public and private.
\[ \text{Price}_{t} = E[\text{Price}_{t+1} \mid \mathcal{F}_{t}] \]

Here, \( E[\text{Price}_{t+1} \mid \mathcal{F}_{t}] \) represents the expected price of the asset at time \( t+1 \), given all available information at time \( t \).

Black-Scholes Model

The Black-Scholes model is a mathematical model used for pricing options. It assumes that the stock follows a geometric Brownian motion with constant volatility. This model is foundational in modern financial theory and helps traders evaluate options pricing.

The formula for the price of a European call option \( C \) is given by:

\[ C = S_0 N(d_1) - Xe^{-rt} N(d_2) \] where \[ d_1 = \frac{\ln(S_0 / X) + (r + \sigma^2 / 2)t}{\sigma \sqrt{t}}, \quad d_2 = d_1 - \sigma \sqrt{t} \]

In this formula:

  • \( C \): Call option price
  • \( S_0 \): Current stock price
  • \( X \): Strike price of the option
  • \( r \): Risk-free interest rate
  • \( t \): Time to expiration
  • \( \sigma \): Volatility of the stock
  • \( N(d) \): Cumulative distribution function of the standard normal distribution

The model's assumptions are key: it assumes no dividends are paid out during the life of the option and that markets are efficient, meaning that all available information is reflected in the asset's price.

Arbitrage Pricing Theory

Arbitrage Pricing Theory (APT) is a multi-factor model that describes the relationship between the expected return of an asset and its risk factors. It asserts that the price of an asset can be determined by its exposure to various risk factors. Unlike the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM), which relies on a single factor (market risk), APT considers multiple factors influencing asset prices.

The expected return \( E[R_i] \) of an asset \( i \) is given by:

\[ E[R_i] = R_f + \beta_1 (E[F_1] - R_f) + \beta_2 (E[F_2] - R_f) + \ldots + \beta_n (E[F_n] - R_f) \]

Here:

  • \( R_f \): Risk-free rate
  • \( \beta_n \): Sensitivity of the asset's return to the \( n \)-th factor
  • \( E[F_n] \): Expected return of the \( n \)-th factor

APT relies on the existence of arbitrage opportunities and assumes that investors will exploit these opportunities until they are eliminated, leading to equilibrium prices.

Technical Analysis

Technical analysis involves evaluating securities by analyzing statistics generated by market activity, such as past prices and volume. It is primarily used for short-term trading decisions. Key principles include:

  • Price Discounts Everything: All available information is reflected in the price.
  • Price Moves in Trends: Prices follow trends that persist for a time.
  • History Tends to Repeat Itself: Market patterns repeat over time due to investor psychology.

Fundamental Analysis

Fundamental analysis evaluates a security's intrinsic value by examining related economic and financial factors. This approach looks at the overall economy and industry conditions to make investment decisions. Key elements include:

  • Earnings Reports: Analyzing earnings per share (EPS) and growth rates.
  • Economic Indicators: GDP growth, unemployment rates, and inflation.
  • Valuation Ratios: Price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio, price-to-book (P/B) ratio, etc.

Cox-Rubinstein Model

The Cox-Rubinstein model is a binomial option pricing model that provides a discrete-time approach to option pricing. This model divides the time to expiration into several intervals, allowing for a more accurate option pricing mechanism compared to the Black-Scholes model. The binomial model considers two possible price movements (up or down) at each interval.

\[ C = e^{-rt} \left( pC_u + (1-p)C_d \right) \]

In this formula:

  • \( C \): Current option price
  • \( r \): Risk-free rate
  • \( t \): Time to expiration
  • \( p \): Probability of an upward price movement
  • \( C_u \): Option price if the stock price goes up
  • \( C_d \): Option price if the stock price goes down

Greek Calculations

Greeks are measures of the sensitivity of the price of options to changes in underlying parameters. They are crucial for risk management and trading strategies. Key Greeks include:

  • Delta: Measures the sensitivity of an option's price to changes in the price of the underlying asset.
  • Gamma: Measures the rate of change of delta with respect to changes in the underlying price.
  • Theta: Measures the sensitivity of the price of an option to the passage of time (time decay).
  • Vega: Measures sensitivity to volatility; how much the price of an option changes with a change in volatility of the underlying asset.
  • Rho: Measures sensitivity to the interest rate; how much the price of an option changes with a change in interest rates.

VIX

The VIX, or Volatility Index, is a measure of the market's expectation of future volatility based on options of the S&P 500 index. Often referred to as the "fear index," it tends to rise during periods of market turmoil.

Options Payoff Formulas

The payoff from options can be calculated using simple formulas. For a call option, the payoff at expiration is:

\[ \text{Payoff}_{\text{call}} = \max(S_T - K, 0) \]

For a put option, the payoff is:

\[ \text{Payoff}_{\text{put}} = \max(K - S_T, 0) \]

Where:

  • \( S_T \): Stock price at expiration
  • \( K \): Strike price

Example Portfolio Formulas

A portfolio's expected return can be calculated as:

\[ E[R_p] = w_1E[R_1] + w_2E[R_2] + \ldots + w_nE[R_n] \]

Where:

  • \( E[R_p] \): Expected return of the portfolio
  • \( w_i \): Weight of asset \( i \) in the portfolio
  • \( E[R_i] \): Expected return of asset \( i \)

Hidden Markov Models

Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) are statistical models that represent systems that transition between hidden states over time. They are widely used in various fields, including finance, for modeling stock price movements. HMMs assume that the system is a Markov process with unobserved states. The basic components include:

  • States: The hidden states that the system can be in.
  • Observations: The observable outputs that are influenced by the hidden states.
  • Transition Probabilities: The probabilities of moving from one state to another.
  • Emission Probabilities: The probabilities of observing certain outputs from each state.

The key equations for HMMs are:

\[ P(O \mid \lambda) = \sum_{q} P(O \mid q, \lambda) P(q \mid \lambda) \]

Where:

  • \( O \): Observed sequence
  • \( \lambda \): Model parameters
  • \( q \): Hidden states

HMMs provide a powerful framework for analyzing time series data and can be employed in stock price prediction and algorithmic trading strategies.
